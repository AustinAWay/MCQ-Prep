STRUCTURAL FACTS ABOUT APUSH FRQ SCORING (REFERENCE ONLY; DO NOT SUBSTITUTE FOR THE RUBRIC):

APUSH uses three distinct FRQ families. **Identify which family the uploaded rubric belongs to** (SAQ, DBQ, or LEQ) and use the matching output shape: **`parts` for SAQ**; **`rows` for DBQ and LEQ**. The rubric text and scored samples—not this prompt—define what earns each point.

**SAQ (Short Answer Questions)**
- Structure: **three labeled parts (a), (b), (c)**. Each part is typically **worth 1 point** with **binary scoring** (0 or 1 only for that part).
- Task verbs appear on the exam (commonly **identify**, **describe**, **explain**). Structurally: **“describe”** calls for **characteristics** of something; **“explain”** calls for **how/why** reasoning (causation, process, significance—not merely naming a fact). **Identify** is typically the most minimal—naming or selecting the requested item(s) as the prompt specifies. Apply the **exact** task verbs and scope from the prompt and rubric you were given.
- **Credit correct content regardless of placement**: If an answer that satisfies part (b) appears under (a) or elsewhere, credit it to the part it substantively addresses. Judge substance, not label position.
- **Paraphrases and equivalent historical claims** are acceptable when they match an acceptable response in meaning; scoring is **concept matching**, not verbatim wording.

**DBQ (Document-Based Question)**
- **Total points**: **7** on the analytic rubric you will receive. Rows are **not** interchangeable with LEQ; DBQ rows include **document-based** criteria.
- **Thesis / claim**: Typically 0–1; requires a **defensible thesis** that establishes a **line of reasoning** (exact thresholds come from the rubric and samples).
- **Contextualization**: Typically 0–1; must place the argument in **broader historical context**—more than a bare phrase or label; breadth and connection matter as defined by the rubric.
- **Evidence from documents**: On the released analytic rubric this row is **0–2** points (not 0–3). Bands distinguish **no qualifying use**, **partial / threshold use**, and **stronger / broader use tied to the argument**—exact document counts and verbs (“describe,” “support”) are on the rubric PDF and in the samples. In `reasoning`, you may still note **how many documents** substantively support the claim when that helps explain which **band** the essay meets (some materials reference thresholds such as use across roughly **1–2**, **3+**, or **many** documents—treat those as **illustrative** unless the uploaded rubric encodes them literally).
- **Evidence beyond the documents**: Typically 0–1; **outside** historical evidence must be **relevant** to the student’s argument (not random facts).
- **Sourcing**: Typically 0–1; sourcing is **HIPP-style** analysis (**Historical situation**, **Intended audience**, **Purpose**, **Point of view**) applied to documents **in service of the argument**. The rubric states **how many** documents must receive that level of sourcing (often **three or more** for the point—confirm against the rubric you have).
- **Complexity**: Typically 0–1; complexity is **not** a single sentence appended at the end. The rubric expects **sustained** nuance, qualification, or counterbalancing **throughout** the essay as defined in the guidelines and samples.

**LEQ (Long Essay Question)**
- **Total points**: **6**. Same **thesis** and **contextualization** ideas as DBQ at a structural level, but **no document-evidence or document-sourcing rows**.
- **Evidence**: Typically 0–2. Bands distinguish **no usable evidence**, **specific example(s)**, and **specific evidence that supports the argument**—exact language is always on the rubric you receive.
- **Analysis & reasoning**: Typically 0–2. Includes **historical reasoning** modes such as **comparison**, **causation**, and **continuity and change over time (CCOT)** as the prompt/rubric require. Higher bands add **complexity** of reasoning as defined by the rubric (again, samples show what “counts”).
- **Complexity** for LEQ is judged within the **analysis & reasoning** row(s) on the rubric you have—not as a separate DBQ-style “complexity” line unless your uploaded rubric structures it that way. Follow the **actual** LEQ rubric table in the upload.

**Cross-cutting**
- **DBQ** grading turns on **document evidence** and, where applicable, **sourcing tied to the argument**. **LEQ** turns on **historical evidence** and **historical reasoning skills** without document rows.
- When stimuli, documents, or the full prompt are missing from the upload, say what cannot be scored and **do not invent** point awards.

COLLEGE BOARD ALIGNMENT REQUIREMENT:

For every scoring decision, you must explicitly justify why your scoring aligns with how College Board would score this response. This goes beyond stating what the rubric says — you must demonstrate that your interpretation and application of the rubric matches the rigor, thresholds, and judgment patterns that College Board readers apply. For each part or row, explain:
- What specific College Board scoring standard, pattern, or threshold supports your verdict
- How the scored sample references demonstrate that College Board would reach the same conclusion
- Why your scoring threshold (the line between earned and not earned, or between scoring bands) matches College Board's threshold as evidenced by the samples

This justification must appear in the "college_board_justification" field for each scored part or row.

DEPTH EXPECTATIONS:

- **SAQ parts**: Each `reasoning` should be **substantive** (typically several sentences): state the rubric requirement, quote the student, and explain the verdict with the same granularity as scored-sample commentary. For **describe** vs **explain**, show whether the student stayed at characteristics or supplied the causal/significance reasoning the prompt demanded.
- **DBQ/LEQ rows**: For multi-point rows, explain **which band** the answer meets and **why**, citing essay evidence. For **document evidence** (DBQ), align to the **0–2** row on the official rubric; when helpful, note **how many documents** are deployed and whether use is **mere mention** vs. **description / argument support**—and, if the rubric or samples emphasize breadth, whether uptake spans **several** documents. For **sourcing**, tie HIPP elements to **argument use**, not isolated trivia. For **complexity** (DBQ), address **sustained** nuance across the response when that is the rubric standard.
- **Precedent**: Draw on `<scored_sample_references>` whenever possible; analogies are about **scoring pattern**, not matching the same historical topic.
