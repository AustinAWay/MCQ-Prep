Question 2: Short Answer Primary Source 
3 points 
 
General Scoring Notes  
• 
Each point is earned independently. 
• 
Accuracy: These scoring guidelines require that students demonstrate historically defensible content 
knowledge. Given the timed nature of the exam, responses may contain errors that do not detract from 
their overall quality, as long as the historical content used to advance the argument is accurate. 
• 
Clarity: Exam responses should be considered first drafts and thus may contain grammatical errors. 
Those errors will not be counted against a student unless they obscure the successful demonstration of 
the content knowledge, skills, and practices described below. 
• 
Describe: Provide the relevant characteristics of a specified topic. Description requires more than 
simply mentioning an isolated term. 
• 
Explain: Provide information about how or why a historical development or process occurs or how or 
why a relationship exists.   
 
[a] 
Briefly describe one historical situation in which the image was created. 
 
  
1 point 
 
Examples that earn this point include the following: 
• 
There was mass unemployment during the Great Depression. 
• 
The New Deal created new federal government programs to aid in recovery from the 
Great Depression. 
• 
Franklin Delano Roosevelt was elected president and passed many new government 
programs. 
[b] 
Briefly explain how the image reflected a change in ideas about the role of government 
from 1877 to 1936. 
 
 
1 point 
 
 
 
 
 
Examples that earn this point include the following: 
• 
In 1877, government economic policy was laissez-faire, and by 1936 the government 
was more involved in the economy. 
• 
During the Progressive Era many Americans argued for a more active government 
role in the economy. 
• 
The stock market crash and the Great Depression convinced many Americans that 
government intervention was necessary. 
• 
Herbert Hoover encouraged voluntary charitable efforts to address the Great 
Depression as opposed to government programs. 
• 
The government allowed boom and bust cycles caused by the industrial economy as 
opposed to intervening during economic downturns.

Short Answer Question 2 
Note: Student samples are quoted verbatim and may contain spelling and grammatical errors. 
Overview 
• 
Responses were expected to describe one historical situation in which a poster promoting the 
Social Security Administration was implemented. 
• 
Responses were expected to explain how the poster reflected a change in ideas about the role 
of government between 1877 and 1936. 
• 
Responses were expected to explain how beliefs about the role of government such as the one 
reflected by the poster continued after 1945. 
• 
The question primarily addressed Topics 6.1, 6.6, 6.12, 6.14, 7.4, 7.8, 7.9, 7.10, 8.4, 8.9, and 
8.15. 
• 
Skills needed to respond to this question were Historical Thinking Skills 2 and 5. 
Sample: 2A 
Score: 3 
The response to (a) earned 1 point because it accurately describes how the relief efforts of the New 
Deal to combat the Great Depression was a historical situation in which the Social Security Act was 
created. 
The response to (b) earned 1 point because it explains the change in ideas about the role of 
government from laissez-faire policies to the direct intervention by the government during the Great 
Depression. 
The response to (c) earned 1 point because it explains how the Great Society continued the belief 
about an active role for the government in the economy by providing direct relief to people through 
programs such as Medicare and Medicaid. 
Sample Identifier: 2B 
Score: 2 
The response to (a) earned 1 point because it accurately describes how Franklin Roosevelt’s New 
Deal in response to the Great Depression was a historical situation of the image. 
The response to (b) earned 1 point because it explains the shift from the laissez-faire government 
policies of the nineteenth and early twentieth century to the government expanding its role and 
becoming “more involved” in the economy. 
The response to (c) did not earn a point because it inaccurately explains Reaganomics as 
representing the idea of expanding the role of government in the economy. Although the response 
states that “social security still exists today,” it does not sufficiently explain how this reflected a 
continuation after 1945 of beliefs about the role of government such as the one reflected by the 
image.
